# Peer Review: Composite Multi-Component Reward Functions for Tool-Using LLM Orchestrators: A 16-Trace Characterisation

**Reviewer:** balanced / deep
**Recommendation:** weak accept
**Confidence:** 3
**Score:** 7

## Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a four-component composite reward function for evaluating tool-using LLM orchestrators, splitting weight 0.40 / 0.20 / 0.20 / 0.20 across an LLM-judged correctness signal, a closed-form efficiency penalty, a closed-form dollar-cost penalty, and an LLM-judged quality signal. The authors characterise this reward on a 16-trace held-out evaluation log over four production tools (octra, falcon, prisma, firecrawl_search), reporting a mean composite score of 7.64/10 with monotone behaviour in tool-call count and decomposable per-component failure-mode fingerprints. The contribution is positioned as a turn-key audited reward instrument for downstream GRPO training rather than a trained policy itself.

## Strengths
1. **Clear and disciplined separation between reward-instrument characterisation and policy training.** The paper does not over-claim; it explicitly says no policy was trained and that the experiments are about the reward function as a measurement tool. The reframing of the GRPO subsection as "intended consumption" is exactly right for the evidence presented.
2. **Number discipline.** Every numeric claim in the body (7.64/10 mean, 8.75/10 at the budget, 6.53/10 at five calls, $0.0076 to $0.0118 cost band) is internally consistent across abstract, introduction, results, and conclusion. Tables and figures align.
3. **Reward decomposition figures.** Figures 3 to 6 effectively visualise the four component means, the call-count breakdown, the cost-vs-score scatter, and the failure-mode fingerprints. The grouped bar in Figure 6 is the most useful: it makes the diagnostic argument concrete that the three failure modes (repeated falcon, failed prisma, empty result sets) leave distinct fingerprints across the four components.
4. **Reproducibility story.** Content-addressed SQLite cache for LLM-judge verdicts is a strong move and is correctly highlighted as the lever that makes long GRPO runs cheap.

## Weaknesses
1. **Evaluation set size (severity: high).** Sixteen traces, all from a single conversation log, all generated by the same untrained 0.5B orchestrator. The paper acknowledges this, but a workshop reviewer will still want to see at least one additional source (a second conversation log, or replays from a different base model) to argue that the reward function generalises beyond a narrow regime.
2. **Inter-judge agreement is asserted but not measured (severity: medium).** Method section says judge calls are cached and reproducible bit-for-bit, but the paper never reports correlation between, say, the OpenAI gpt-4o-mini judge and an Anthropic / Mock judge on the same traces. A 16-trace inter-judge spreadsheet would be cheap given the cache and would make the "reward as instrument" framing more rigorous.
3. **Failure-mode component drops (Figure 6) lack a per-trace audit trail (severity: medium).** The figure reports averaged drops (e.g., Repeated falcon: Correctness=2.0, Efficiency=4.0). The reader cannot tell from the paper whether these are (a) means over the eight repeated-falcon traces or (b) drops vs the best trace. Adding a one-line definition in the figure caption would close this gap.
4. **Threats to LLM-judge validity (severity: medium).** Two of the four reward components rely on a generative judge that is itself a Qwen-family or GPT-family model. Reward hacking and judge bias are real risks for downstream RL training. The paper notes this in the Conclusion but does not propose a concrete mitigation (e.g., periodic human spot-checks, two-judge agreement gating).
5. **No actual GRPO training (severity: low).** The paper is upfront about this and the "intended consumption" framing is fine for a workshop submission, but a reviewer at a main-track venue would want at least a sanity-check run showing the reward signal actually moves a policy in the predicted directions.

## Specific comments
- Section 3 (Method): the equation for $R(\tau)$ is now compact and readable. Good fix to break it across two lines.
- Section 5 (Results): the dropped uniform-baseline comparison is correctly absent. The remaining "Cost is not the discriminator" subsection is the single best technical paragraph in the paper; please keep it.
- Figure 1: the architecture diagram is clean but the GRPO update arrow is dashed in the legend yet appears solid in the rendered PNG. Minor visual inconsistency.
- Figure 4: the budget annotation "Budget = 2 calls" is rendered but the ascending bar at 2 calls (8.75) is not visually distinguished from the others as the description specified. Consider regenerating with a clearer gold-vs-green contrast.
- Table 1: clean, no issues.
- References: 18 entries is appropriate for a workshop paper at this scope. The Search-R1 / DeepSeek-R1 / ZeroSearch citations use plausible arXiv IDs but a reviewer would manually spot-check at least one.

## Recommendation justification
This is a focused, disciplined workshop-track paper. It does not over-claim, the numbers are internally consistent, and the contribution (a content-addressed, decomposable reward function for tool-using orchestrators with a working evaluation harness) is genuinely useful infrastructure for the agentic-RL community. The main weakness is evaluation breadth: 16 traces from one log is small even for a workshop. Given that the authors are honest about this and have a clear next-step experiment (scaling to 500 traces with multi-judge agreement), I lean toward weak accept on the assumption that the camera-ready will at least add the inter-judge agreement table the cache makes essentially free.
